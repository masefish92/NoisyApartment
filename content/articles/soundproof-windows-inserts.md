Windows are the weakest point in most apartments for street noise, barking dogs, and traffic. The fix isn't a curtain, it's adding a second sealed layer of air and glass (or acrylic) between you and the noise. Here are the three options that actually do that, ranked by cost.

## Why windows leak so much noise

A single pane of glass has almost no mass and no air gap to slow sound down. The fix used in every method below is the same principle: trap a layer of dead air between two rigid surfaces. That air gap is what actually blocks sound, not the material itself.

## Option 1: DIY acrylic secondary glazing

The most praised budget hack is cutting a sheet of quarter-inch (or thicker) acrylic slightly smaller than the window opening, then sealing it into the frame with silicone weatherstripping so it wedges in tight. The trapped air between your window and the acrylic sheet becomes an effective secondary acoustic barrier.

**Do this:** measure the window opening precisely, order acrylic cut to size, and use suction-cup handles so you can remove the panel when you want to open the window.

## Option 2: Professional window inserts

Custom-fit inserts like Indow Windows seal tightly against the existing window frame without any permanent changes. They're frequently described as a complete fix for high-frequency noise (voices, barking, traffic hum) and install and remove without tools or damage.

This costs significantly more than the DIY option, but it's the closest thing to "install it and forget about the noise" available to renters.

## Option 3: Weatherstripping only

If a full insert isn't in the budget, sealing the gaps around an old or poorly fitted window frame with adhesive foam weatherstripping tape closes the air leaks sound travels through. It's the cheapest option here, but it only helps if your window already has visible gaps, it won't add mass to the glass itself.

## FAQ

**Do soundproof curtains work as well as a window insert?**
No. Curtains help slightly with echo and light sound absorption, but they can't replicate the sealed air gap an acrylic panel or insert creates. See our [soundproof curtains guide](/blog/do-soundproof-curtains-work) for what curtains can and can't do.

**Will an acrylic panel stop me from opening my window?**
Not if you install it with suction-cup handles instead of permanent sealant. You lose a few seconds of removal time in exchange for a fully reversible, deposit-safe install.

**How much noise reduction can I realistically expect?**
Most renters report a clearly noticeable drop in mid and high-frequency noise (traffic hum, voices, barking) with either the DIY panel or a professional insert. Very low bass and heavy impact noise are harder to stop through glass alone.

**Is this worth doing if I'm only renting for a year?**
The DIY acrylic panel is the better call for a short lease, since it costs less and removes cleanly. Professional inserts make more financial sense the longer you plan to stay.

## The renter's bottom line

If your budget is tight, the acrylic panel gets you most of the benefit for a fraction of the cost. If you're staying for years and the noise is a real problem, a professional insert is the closest thing to solved. Either way, seal the gaps first, that's the cheapest step and it compounds with whatever else you add.
